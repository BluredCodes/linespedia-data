That which is perfect

must be so small and occupy 

hardly any space at all.

It must be short, and quick

and pass very fast.

The longer something lasts

and the bigger it is

the more likely it will develop flaws--

more things can go wrong.

It's more vulnerable to the world around.



That which is perfect

would already contain

in its present state

its absolute future.

It would neither improve

nor decline over time.

It would contain in its present

its absolute ultimate result.



Finally, for it to be known, 

it would have to exist in

a comparative, contrasting relation

to that which was imperfect. 



--Uncle Greg
